Getting Started: Motion Blocks

snap gives you eight categories of blocks: Motion, Looks, Sound.... Right now, the blue "Motion" tab is lit up at the top of the page and the palette below shows the blocks in that category.

  1. Click on three or four times and watch the stage (the large white region at the upper right of your window) to see what the arrowhead-shaped sprite does.
  2. Click on a few times to see what happens.
  3. What does do?
  4. What do the other blocks seem to do?
  5. Change the numbers in some of the blocks and see how that changes what the block does.
Why is this called "pen down" instead of "leave a trace"?
When a robot is doing the drawing, a real pen is either down (touching the page) or up (not touching the paper). In this picture, you can see the red pen through the robot turtle's transparent green shell.
Valiant Technology Ltd. [CC BY-SA 3.0], via Wikimedia Commons

The sprite moved without leaving a trace. If you want the sprite to draw—to leave a trace as it moves—it must "touch the tip of its pen" to the "paper" of the stage. The command tells it to set its penpoint down on that paper. Find in the green "Pen" tab, click it, then come back to the "Motion" tab, and try some of the move, turn, and go to x:_ y:_ blocks again.

  1. Click the other tabs to find these thirteen blocks. As you find them, click them to figure out what they do. Some have effects on the stage. Some do other things.
  2. As you've seen, blocks in the "Motion" tab correspond to motion-like actions. Drag the block to some spot in the scripting area and drop it there.

  3. You already know that if you click on that block, the sprite will move that number of steps. You also know that you can vary the input of the block to change the number of steps you want to the sprite to move. Experiment to figure out how to change the block input so that the sprite moves backwards!
