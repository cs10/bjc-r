Former purpose ramblings

This lab does not introduce new ideas, but gives students practice applying MAP, KEEP, and COMBINE in several contexts, in order to master the use of these powerful tools and to learn to think in terms of what they can do.

Lists of same-size lists can function as tables or matrices. Tic Tac Toe... Magic Squares gives students a game context in which to practice further their use of lists and list processing functions.

Anything that can be handled by creating and manipulating a list can also be done without lists, just by working with the individual elements and keeping track of that process. But list processing—especially list processing with the higher order functions map, keep, and combine (and, later in this course, recursion)—is powerful. That is because it lets the programmer focus on the purpose and meaning of the program—what the program is intended to do—and not on tiny local details of how to do it, details that essentially amount to inventing from scratch a clumsy equivalent of lists and ways to work sequentially through them.

Becoming facile with list processing requires practice, and initially requires contexts in which the meaning of the lists and the purpose and meaning of the functions that process them are clear. One subtlety of working with lists is that it requires thinking simultaneously about the list as a single object and, at the same time, recognizing that much of what one wants to do with the list involves processing some or all of the elements of the list. That is also part of the subtlety of the higher-order functions, which take an entire list as a single object and handle the individual elements of the list in various ways, as determined by the programmer. Composing higher order functions is yet more powerful, but also more challenging, because each one does so much that the programmer needs to picture large effects and the interactions of these large effects.

This lab gives students practice applying map, keep, and combine in several contexts to build their facility with these powerful tools and to learn to think in terms of what they can do. It does not introduce new ideas, but focuses on mastery and application.

Students will:

  • Practice combining higher order functions to perform more complicated list manipulations.
