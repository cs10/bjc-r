Programming Lab 1: Conditional Blocks

In this lab, students focus attention on conditionals—especially if-else and if—to control the behavior of their programs. Students learn to use predicates (blocks that report true or false based on tests they perform) such as < or =, and learn to build other special purpose predicates. Early on, they test the direction and y position of the sprite, and base actions on the results.

Introducing conditionals. An ATM is programmed to let you choose whether to deposit or withdraw. The program does that by placing those choices somewhere on its screen and then testing to determine where you touch or which button you press. The action the program takes or the choice it offers next is conditional; it depends on what input you've given it with your touch. Another part of the program may test the size of the withdrawal you request, and indicate if that is too large, or not an amount it can give; or it may deliver that amount. Your smart phone's screen works a similar way. It knows only where you touch, but because it also knows what word or picture it put in that location, it can "know what you want"—if you touch here, do one thing, else do something else. Distinguishing conditions and basing action on the distinction is central to all programming.

Small projects focus especially on using multiple if statements to distinguish multiple conditions. Students design a program that tests to see whether "red," "yellow," or "green" has been typed, and sets a traffic signal to that color; they design another program to decide when to add s and when to add es to make the plural of a noun.

This lab also begins to focus on abstraction through analyzing tasks to break them into subtasks and then creating blocks that specialize in these subtasks, and through analyzing and debugging scripts. Analyzing an existing script is a particularly powerful way to learn to program similar behaviors. Debugging is an essential skill—especially as one's programs become more complex—but it takes time to acquire and hone that skill because there is no single formula or subskill for doing it well. In this lab, the debugging focus is on deliberately trying to "break" a program, searching for ways that a user's input (or some situation that arises on the stage) can cause a program to fail, and accounting for those ways by testing for them and responding appropriately.

Looking Ahead

The Take-It-Further on the last page of this lab, Plurals, continued, foreshadows recursion, a powerful idea that students will study in Unit 6. Students develop plural to spell the plural of an English noun, creating separate blocks for various special cases (nouns ending with s, x, h, y, and so on) and otherwise just appending s. Then they fix plural to handle a "word ending with a space" and use plural, itself, as the specialist to handle the word after that space has been removed. It may surprise them to see that this solution works even with "words" that end with many spaces.

  • Analyze scripts to understand how they work and to learn to use those methods for their own scripts.
  • Develop predicates to test for various conditions and use conditional blocks to control programs based on those conditions.
  • Analyze tasks to find subtasks that can be handled independently, develop blocks that specialize in each of the subtasks, and use conditionals to determine which of these specialist blocks to use.
  • Use abstraction to simplify and clarify code.
  • Debug code by deliberately trying to find ways to "break" it.

Tips:

  • Page 1: Getting started. The "getting started" section in any lab involves some pure experimentation (students use code to see what it does) and some puzzles. Some of the puzzles are hard. Let students know that if they've done the experiments, the playing around, they have most of the knowledge they need for the puzzles but puzzles are puzzling! Try, tinker, try something new, talk to your partner. And if you can't solve the puzzle yourself, that's ok. Talk to another group.
  • Make sure that students don't skip over the "figure out how it works" part of problem 1 on the "Getting Started" page. In this case, the figuring out helps students do problems 2 and 3 on that page. It's probably not necessary to do anything formal with that "figure out" part—talking with the pair programmer is likely to be enough—but if you feel your students need more structure, you might prepare a paper copy of the scripts for students to annotate, or some other way to make the results of problem 1 more tangible than discussion.
  • Page 2: Creating predicates. Problem 2 uses a video to show how to crete the even? block. If you have a projector, it may be easier for your students to watch you demonstrate and copy what you do in snap, instead of trying to have both the student guide and the snap page open on their machine. For the rest of the problems on the page, avoid demonstrating. Students will learn most by puzzling them out. Take-It-Further challenge B is particularly worthwhile for students who have the time.
  • Page 3: Using multiple if statements. If time is short, have students do only the first three problems on this page. There will be other opportunities to learn what they will skip here.
  • Pages 4 and 5: Plurals. The prior pages were introduction and preparation. This project is core. Students learn how to do something more complex with significant sub-parts, a task that requires analysis, experimentation, and debugging.
    • All students (but especially those whose English is not native or not "standard") may have questions about what is the correct plural or correct way to spell it. Encourage them to feel free to check with each other, but also feel free to ask you. Nobody can possibly think of all possible words anyway, so let students feel that it is okay simply to ignore ones that they're not yet sure about. One side-benefit of this project is that it helps student notice how much they do know and take for granted, and how much work it is for a computer to do what humans do naturally! Students may do this kind of activity in another language if they like. The Take It Further on page 4 suggests one such investigation.
    • The following rule summaries are for your benefit. It's best if you can avoid teaching them to students; let them discover the rules. You can contribute by "torpedoing" a specialist block they think they've finished by proposing a word it gets wrong.
      • plural-h needs to recognize words that end with ch and sh so that it can join es to those. To all other word (for example, ones that end with th), we just join s. To do this, plural-h must look at the next-to-last letter of the word.
      • plural-y also needs to look at the next-to-last letter to see if it is a vowel. To words like day and boy with vowels before the y, we just join s. For the others, like spy and fly, we "change" the last letter (y) to i (by removing the y and using join to replace it with an i) and then join es.
    • The Take-it-further contains a wonderful surprise that foreshadows a powerful programming technique students will learn in unit 6. All Take-it-furthers are optional but this one is especially worthwhile, and not difficult. If there is time, encourage students to try it. But don't make a big fuss about it; the goal is to sneak in a taste of recursion before anyone tells them it's hard to learn. It's best if you don't even mention the word "recursion" at this point.
