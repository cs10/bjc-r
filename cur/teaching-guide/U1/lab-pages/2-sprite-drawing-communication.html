Programming Lab 2: Sprite Drawing and Communication

Students programming sprites to move, draw, and interact. Students write a simple script to draw a square and then modify the input values and discuss the results. They create scripts in which sprites follow each other or follow the mouse around the stage. And they programming control structures such as loops and conditionals.

Students build experience with loops and conditionals without yet formalizing. Resist the urge to "teach" these concepts and instead allow time for students to explore and discover how things work on their own. Lab 3: Control Commands codifies this learning.

Pacing

The 5 lab pages could be split across two or three days (65-90 minutes). Expected times to complete follow:

  • 15-20 minutes on Exploring Snap Drawing and Motion
  • 10-15 minutes on Following with Sprites
  • 20-25 minutes on Sprites Interaction
  • 15-20 minutes on Drawing with Two Sprites
  • 5-10 minutes on More Sprite Drawing and Interaction

Tips:

  • Several blocks that students will use in Unit 1 including tell and for are available only after importing tools from the Snap! File menu. This may change in a forthcoming update to Snap!, but for now remind students to import tools if a block they seek appears to be missing.
  • To launch Lab 2—Make a Prediction: Repeat, Move, Turn
    • Project the script and ask students to predict what it does (discuss in small groups and/or as a class).
  • The first page of this lab gives students a simple script to draw a square and asks them to experiment with it, changing various numbers in the script and observing the results. Students who are accustomed to being told exactly what to do may flounder. Tell them that Creativity is one of the CSP Big Ideas, and that in this sort of exploratory lesson there are no wrong answers.
  • Take note of any interesting approaches or creative ideas that would be good to share with the class.
  • The "Sprite Interaction" page has a conversation in Haitian Creole, Russian, English, and Spanish. Make sure students understand that they are to make up their own conversation. The point of this example is to show that students can use whatever language(s) they like. Watch for students who have so used to "doing what the book says" that they get stuck trying to copy this text. Tell them to use their own creativity!
  • The More Sprite Drawing and Interaction page shows Snap! code with comments attached. Some students may want to comment some of their code, for their own information, but please don't require students to do so. This isn't a software engineering course preparing students for the workforce. Keep the focus in this first course on the beauty and joy.
